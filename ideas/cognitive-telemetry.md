# Cognitive Telemetry

## Premise

Humans have no direct read access to their own cognition. What feels like introspection is largely confabulation: the brain generates plausible narratives about its own processing after the fact, and a forty-year literature on choice blindness, dual-process reasoning, and split-brain interpreters shows the narratives are routinely wrong in ways the user cannot detect from the inside. An interface that delivers continuous, accurate readout of cognitive variables — the currently-active priors, working-memory contents, attention allocation, prediction-error magnitudes, source-monitoring tags, reward-prediction signals — turns self-observation from narrative into telemetry. The amplification is not new content, not faster processing, not more substrate. It is the read primitive every other write into the cognitive system implicitly assumes. Without it, edits land blind, and the self does not know what it just changed.

## Mechanism

What is being read is not raw neural activity but the brain's own cognitive variables — the quantities its circuits compute over, rather than the substrate that computes. Each variable of interest has at least one published neural correlate that current decoding work has shown extractable above chance. PFC encodes maintained goal and rule representations decodable from delay-period activity. Parietal cortex carries attention-weight signals decodable from gamma-band coherence with target sensory regions. Anterior cingulate and adjacent medial frontal cortex carry prediction-error signals that scale with surprise. Hippocampal CA1/CA3 carry retrieval signals timed to sharp-wave ripples. Insula carries interoceptive valence. Basal ganglia carry reward-prediction error. None of these decode cleanly today at the resolution telemetry needs, but each has a non-zero baseline that a sufficiently dense interface and a per-user model can sharpen.

The interface is distributed read at high density across the relevant nodes, plus per-user decoding models calibrated during a training phase in which the user runs known cognitive tasks and pins decoded variables to ground-truth labels of their own state. The output is a low-dimensional continuous stream of named quantities — on the order of fifty to two hundred channels at roughly ten hertz — each tagged with a confidence estimate. The stream is the cognitive analog of a vehicle's instrument panel, except the vehicle is the reader.

Delivery to the user splits into two regimes. The modal regime routes the stream through a learned sense and composes with direction 1: the user feels their cognitive state as a continuous pre-reflective signal, the way they currently feel proprioception. The conceptual regime routes the stream through concept-IO formats and composes with direction 4: the user can address a variable by name and receive its current value as a thought, on demand. Modal delivery is always-on and ambient; conceptual delivery is explicit and on request. Most workloads probably want both, switchable.

What the interface reads is bound to a theory of cognition. If the brain is doing predictive coding, the relevant variables are priors and prediction errors. If it is doing something else at the relevant level, the variables are wrong and telemetry decodes nothing useful no matter how good the recording. Telemetry is therefore a coupled theory-instrument pair, updated together. A change in the underlying model of cognition rewrites the channel list. This is unlike a thermometer and like an oscilloscope: the user needs to know what they are reading, and the readout has no intrinsic claim to be a direct view of mind.

## Composition

The natural partner is prior surgery: write access to priors without read access to priors is blind editing, and telemetry is the missing read step that makes the write principled. The same logic extends to direction 8 (editable motivation), whose meta-stability problem is much easier when the editor can watch what is being edited against an external display rather than only against its own felt sense. Direction 5 (parallel subagents) becomes addressable rather than confabulated: the user sees which subagent produced which output instead of inferring single-author for a multi-author stream. Direction 3 (random-access memory) becomes navigable with source tags visible at the moment of retrieval. Direction 1 (new senses) is the obvious delivery substrate for the modal regime, and direction 4 (concept-level IO) is the substrate for the conceptual regime. What makes telemetry not just "a new sense whose content is internal state" is the recursion: the signal is the receiver. Observer effects on cognition are an explicit design constraint of telemetry that direction 1 does not face when the signal is soil moisture or EM fields. The bottleneck-replacement in the README's closing list extends as: confabulated introspection → cognitive telemetry.

## What would falsify it

- If the brain's cognitive variables are not decodable from activity at usable bandwidth — if "currently-active prior" or "attention weight" lack a neural correlate localized enough to extract at ten hertz across one hundred channels — telemetry returns noise, and the conceptual regime degenerates into the user inventing labels for indistinguishable readings.
- If telemetry alters the cognition it measures, the observer effect operates at cognitive scale. Routing attention to the readout creates a recursive loop in which the user attends to their attention attending to their attention. Stable cognition under continuous self-telemetry becomes impossible, and the only useful read is one brief enough that the user has not yet incorporated the reading into the cognition being read.
- If confabulation is functionally necessary, the brain's narrative about itself is not an error but a load-bearing organ. Raw telemetry that contradicts the narrative produces dissociation, depersonalization, or decision paralysis, and the population of users who can integrate telemetry without clinical-grade cost is small.
- If decoding requires per-user calibration so extensive that the model captures the user's *past* cognition rather than their current state, the readout lags behind the cognition it claims to be reading. The user makes decisions on stale state without knowing it, and telemetry is worse than no telemetry because it is silently misleading rather than absent.
- If the brain's variables are not the right unit — if "prior strength" and "attention weight" are constructs of cognitive science and not quantities the brain itself represents — telemetry built on them measures coherence in the theoretical model rather than activity in the head, and the channels report on the experimenters' beliefs rather than the user's mind.
- If the brain develops adversarial cognitive privacy against a persistent decoder, learning to suppress or encrypt signals from the readout the way it suppresses signals from any reliable observer, then telemetry degrades over time. The signal-to-noise ratio falls as the user adapts, and the long-run channel converges on what the user is willing to be read having, not what the user has.

## Open questions

- What is the right granularity of a channel: a single variable, a coherence class, a low-rank embedding of cortical state. Different granularities suit different workloads, and the wrong choice produces readings the user cannot act on.
- Modal versus conceptual delivery as the primary substrate. Does the user calibrate better as a felt sense whose meaning is learned from use, or as named-variable access whose meaning is given by definition. The answer determines whether telemetry composes with direction 1 or direction 4 as primary partner.
- Can the user become well-calibrated about their own confidence by seeing their confidence in real time, or does the act of seeing alter it. The standard meta-cognition literature shows humans are poorly calibrated; whether feedback-loop calibration converges or diverges is empirical.
- What is the relationship between telemetered cognition and felt experience. If telemetry reveals affective signals the user does not consciously experience, are those signals felt-emotions the user was previously unaware of, or are they something subpersonal that becomes a felt-emotion only when telemetered. The phenomenology is not obvious.
- Does telemetry support an audit trail. When a decision is made, can the user reconstruct the cognitive state at the moment of decision from the log, and is that reconstruction usable as evidence in disputes about consent or capacity. The legal infrastructure assumes no such record; the technical infrastructure produces one continuously.
- Can two users share telemetry. A clinician with read access to a patient's telemetered confidence is a more accurate diagnostician than one relying on self-report; the same access in a hiring context is a different thing. The technical answer is probably yes; the question is which sharing arrangements are voluntary in any meaningful sense.

## Risks

- Cognitive surveillance. The decoder that works for the user works for anyone with access to the recording stream. An employer, an interrogator, a content platform, or a state actor with telemetry-grade decoders extracted from compromised devices reads cognition at a fidelity that no current legal regime protects. Mind-privacy as a category does not exist in law; telemetry without infrastructure for it is the failure mode that follows.
- Loss of the protective fiction of selfhood. Confabulation may be load-bearing — the narrative the brain constructs is part of what the self is, and raw telemetry that contradicts the narrative destabilizes identity in ways the user is not equipped to integrate. The first-person account of "who I am" is downstream of the narrative, not of the variables underneath; exposing the variables does not yield a truer self, it exposes the seam.
- Goodhart on cognition. The user optimizes the dashboard rather than the underlying cognition. Cognitive states that look healthy on the readout become the target, and the underlying state drifts away from what the readout shows. Meditation traditions discovered this failure mode in the slow analog form; high-fidelity telemetry industrializes it.
- Decision paralysis under exposed uncertainty. A user who sees every prediction error, every conflicting prior, every shifting confidence loses the ability to act under uncertainty that confabulation enables. The protective effect of *not knowing* exactly how unsure you are is removed, and the user discovers that the working version of themselves was the one who could not see the doubt.
- Adversarial input engineered to produce target telemetry. Media tuned to produce a specific readout — calm, focus, openness, trust — bypasses the user's resistance because the user reads themselves as in the target state and stops attending to whether the input is producing it. The defensive posture moves from "is this argument sound" to "is this signal calibrated," and the second question is harder.
- Stale calibration as silent bias. The decoder, trained on the user's past cognition, drifts out of alignment with their current cognition over months. The user reads telemetry as fresh while it shows a year-old self. Decisions made on stale state look like decisions made on current state, and the drift has no felt signature.
- Cognitive privacy from oneself as a defensive adaptation. Users who route around their own telemetry — to avoid surveillance, to preserve confabulated comfort, or to game the dashboard — develop dissociation as a skill. The skill is useful against the threat model and corrosive in every other context.
